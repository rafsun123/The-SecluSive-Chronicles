    Strategies and tips to get all A/A* in A Level Physics, Biology, Mathematics, and Chemistry.

    Everyone has a notion that achieving an A* in A Levels sounds like a daunting task. I think it's not, provided you invest your time with proper tactics and stay consistent. I will repudiate the ideology that obtaining high grades is impossible. I took four subjects in A Levels: Physics, Biology, Mathematics, and Chemistry, and got 3A* and 1A in a single sitting. I am sharing my tips and tricks that I applied to achieve these grades.

    Firstly, after deciding to take A Levels, you might ambivalently wonder the most redundant question: "Is it worth taking numerous subjects in A Levels, such as 5, 6, or even 7?" The answer is both no and yes. Taking fewer or more subjects has its advantages and disadvantages. I will start by highlighting these points. After completing their O Levels or equivalent, students often have the propensity to take many subjects. It's beneficial to take numerous subjects if you want to keep various career pathways open. For instance, if you decide to become a doctor and take Biology, Chemistry, and Physics, but also want to be a computer engineer which might require Maths and Computer Science, then taking five subjects can be advantageous. Additionally, you will gain significant overall knowledge, becoming a more academically well-rounded student. However, taking many subjects also has drawbacks. A common issue is time allocation. Each subject has a vast syllabus with rigorous coursework, making it challenging to cover all subjects within one or two years. Additionally, each subject carries a high expense, and some students might not be able to afford the fees. Nonetheless, if you believe you can manage multiple subjects and invest significant time and money in each, then go for it.

    From my opinion, I think it's ideal to take three or four subjects in A Level based on passion, love, and future major. Read below to ace your A Level effectively

    Tip 1:

    The first task when you start an A Level subject is to review the syllabus. You can find it online by searching for "Cambridge AS & A Level [your subject] syllabus" on Google. While you can buy coursebooks or other textbooks, these often don’t cover everything included in the syllabus. The syllabus is organized into points for each chapter, detailing what you need to recall, understand, or draw. Don’t just read through the syllabus—make sure to understand each point thoroughly.

    Tip 2:

    As you read the syllabus, you might wonder how you'll grasp everything it requires. While the syllabus outlines what you need to know for the exam, it won’t necessarily strengthen your understanding of the concepts. To build a solid understanding of the topics, you should use various resources, such as textbooks, teacher lectures, and notes from teachers and friends. Today, resources like notes and lectures are widely available on the internet. For example, you can find valuable materials on platforms like YouTube, Z-notes, and Weebly.

    Tip 3:

    Building a strong understanding of concepts is crucial for achieving good grades in any subject. However, retaining and recalling information long-term can be challenging, unless you're exceptionally gifted. Therefore, the best approach is to create your own notes. Avoid making them vague by merely copying text from books. Instead, develop your own unique style—record your knowledge and concepts in a way that makes sense to you. Use bullet points to cover the key points in the syllabus effectively.

    Tip 4:

    A crucial tip for improving your problem-solving skills and reinforcing your understanding is solving past papers. Aim to work through at least 10 to 15 years’ worth of past papers, including the most recent ones. Reserve at least 20 past papers for mock exams that you take on your own. Solving past papers is a powerful strategy to transform ambiguous concepts into clear, strong knowledge. Begin with topical papers as you complete each section of the syllabus, and then tackle them year by year.

    Tip 5:

    After following the tips above for several months, it’s time to test yourself. Create an environment similar to the exam setting and sit for mock exams within the allotted time. Grade yourself and analyze your mistakes; seek help from teachers or resources like YouTube if needed. Keep a notebook to record all your errors and misconceptions, and use it to guide your revision. Continue this process iteratively until the exam day arrives.

    Tip 6: My final tip

    So, this is the last tip I will suggest. Be consistent and productive; adhere with this routine until your exam. Eat healthy and sleep well, celebrate small victory. If you strive and work hard effectively you can achieve the highest grade.

    I hope this article can stand as an incentive for your preparation.
    Best of Luck.
